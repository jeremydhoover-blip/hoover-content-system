# Examples

## Example 1: Tech company brand alignment

**Input:**
- Brand positioning: "Enterprise-grade power, startup-speed simplicity"
- Brand attributes: Powerful, Simple, Fast, Trustworthy
- Target audience: Growing companies upgrading from basic tools
- Challenge: Balance "enterprise" credibility with "approachable" feel

**Output:**

```md
# Brand-Content Alignment Guide: ScaleUp Platform

## Brand foundation

### Brand positioning
ScaleUp delivers enterprise-grade capabilities with the simplicity and speed growing companies need. We're the tool you won't outgrow.

### Brand promise
Power without complexity.

### Key differentiators
1. Enterprise features without enterprise onboarding
2. Fast implementation (hours, not months)
3. Scales from 10 to 10,000 users without re-platforming

---

## Brand-to-content translation

| Brand attribute | What it means (brand) | What it means (content) | Content example |
|-----------------|----------------------|------------------------|-----------------|
| Powerful | Enterprise capabilities | Show capability without overwhelming | "Handle millions of records" not "robust scalable infrastructure" |
| Simple | Easy to use | Clear, jargon-free, scannable | "Set up in 3 clicks" not "streamlined configuration process" |
| Fast | Quick implementation | Emphasize speed, reduce friction | "Live in hours" not "rapid deployment capabilities" |
| Trustworthy | Reliable, secure | Specific claims, show evidence | "99.9% uptime" not "industry-leading reliability" |

---

## Content behaviors in detail

### Powerful: Show, don't tell

**Brand says:** Enterprise-grade capabilities
**Content does:** Demonstrates capability with specifics, not adjectives

**This looks like:**
- Concrete numbers ("10 million rows," "500 integrations")
- Feature comparisons that show capability
- Customer proof points at scale

**This doesn't look like:**
- Vague power words ("robust," "powerful," "comprehensive")
- Enterprise jargon ("leverage," "optimize," "utilize")
- Claims without evidence

**Example:**
> âœ“ "Process 10 million records in under a minute"
> âœ— "Robust and scalable data processing capabilities"

---

### Simple: Clarity over cleverness

**Brand says:** Startup-speed simplicity
**Content does:** Uses plain language, reduces cognitive load

**This looks like:**
- Short sentences (under 20 words)
- Common words over technical terms
- One idea per paragraph
- Visual hierarchy for scanning

**This doesn't look like:**
- Dense paragraphs
- Jargon without explanation
- Clever wordplay that obscures meaning

**Example:**
> âœ“ "Connect your data in 3 steps"
> âœ— "Our intuitive onboarding experience facilitates seamless data integration"

---

### Fast: Emphasize speed

**Brand says:** Quick time to value
**Content does:** Highlights speed, reduces perceived effort

**This looks like:**
- Time estimates ("5 minutes," "same day")
- Step counts ("3 steps")
- "Start now" language

**This doesn't look like:**
- Open-ended timelines
- Complex prerequisite lists
- Process-heavy language

**Example:**
> âœ“ "Go live today. Most teams are up in 2 hours."
> âœ— "Begin your digital transformation journey with our onboarding program"

---

### Trustworthy: Prove it

**Brand says:** Reliable and secure
**Content does:** Provides evidence, avoids empty claims

**This looks like:**
- Specific numbers (uptime %, certifications)
- Named customers with permission
- Transparent about limitations

**This doesn't look like:**
- Vague superlatives ("best-in-class," "industry-leading")
- Unsubstantiated claims
- Hiding limitations

**Example:**
> âœ“ "99.9% uptime SLA. SOC 2 Type II certified."
> âœ— "Industry-leading security and reliability"

---

## Content type alignment

| Content type | Primary attribute | Secondary | Special considerations |
|--------------|-------------------|-----------|----------------------|
| Marketing | Simple, Fast | Powerful | Lead with ease, prove with capability |
| Product UI | Simple | Fast | Never sacrifice clarity for brevity |
| Documentation | Trustworthy | Simple | Accuracy first, then clarity |
| Sales | Powerful | Trustworthy | Match to buyer sophistication |

---

## Red flags
- "Leverage," "utilize," "optimize" (jargon alert)
- "Best-in-class," "industry-leading" (empty claims)
- Paragraphs over 3 sentences (complexity creep)
- No numbers in capability claims (missing evidence)
```

- Brand positioning: "Wellness that fits your life"

- Challenge: Be supportive without being preachy

### Encouraging: Support without performing

**Brand says:** Judgment-free support
**Content does:** Acknowledges effort, doesn't oversell enthusiasm

**This looks like:**
- Understated celebration ("Nice work." not "AMAZING!!!")
- Acknowledging that things are hard
- Forward-looking after setbacks

**This doesn't look like:**
- Cheerleader energy (excessive enthusiasm)

### Realistic: Honor constraints

**Brand says:** Fits your life
**Content does:** Acknowledges real-world limitations

**This looks like:**
- Options for different time/energy levels
- Acknowledging that life gets in the way
- Not requiring perfection

**This doesn't look like:**
- One-size-fits-all prescriptions
- Implying failure if you can't do the full program
- Ignoring common obstacles

**Example:**
> âœ“ "Don't have 30 minutes? Here's a 5-minute version that still counts."
> âœ— "Commit to 30 minutes daily for best results."

### Personal: It's about them

**Brand says:** Adapted to the individual
**Content does:** Uses personal language, avoids universal prescriptions

**This looks like:**

- Acknowledging different bodies/needs
- Choices over mandates

**This doesn't look like:**
- "Everyone should..."

- Prescriptive language

**Example:**
> âœ“ "Find what works for your body"
> âœ— "The best morning routine for everyone"

### Science-backed: Evidence, not lectures

**Brand says:** Based on research
**Content does:** Cites evidence without being preachy

**This looks like:**
- Brief research mentions with sources available
- "Research suggests" not "You must"
- Explaining why something works

**This doesn't look like:**
- Dense citations that feel academic
- Lecturing or moralizing
- Pseudoscience or unfounded claims

**Example:**

> âœ— "Studies definitively prove you should walk after every meal for optimal metabolic function."

- Exclamation points (more than 1 per screen = too enthusiastic)
- "You should" / "You need to" (prescriptive)
- Weight loss promises (not our positioning)
- Comparison to others ("Unlike other people who...")
