# Examples

## Example 1: Blog post headline variants

**Input:**
- Content: Article about why engineering teams should adopt pair programming
- Platform: Tech blog
- Constraints: 60-80 characters preferred, SEO important
- Target keyword: "pair programming benefits"
- Audience: Engineering managers and team leads

**Output:**

```md
# Headline variants for: Pair Programming Adoption Article

## Content summary
An argument for pair programming based on code quality metrics, onboarding speed, and team knowledge sharing, with implementation guidance.

## Primary constraints
- **Platform:** Tech blog (web)
- **Max characters:** 70-80 (Google truncates at ~60)
- **Primary goal:** SEO + engagement
- **Target audience:** Engineering managers

---

## Variants

### Benefit-driven
> Pair Programming Cut Our Bug Rate by 40% — Here's How We Did It

**Characters:** 63
**Best for:** Readers looking for proven results
**Why it works:** Leads with concrete outcome, promises actionable content

### Curiosity-driven
> The Counterintuitive Reason Two Engineers Are Faster Than One

**Characters:** 60
**Best for:** Social sharing, engaging skeptics
**Why it works:** Challenges assumption, creates cognitive tension

### Specific/Data-driven
> We Tracked 6 Months of Pair Programming. Here's What the Data Shows.

**Characters:** 66
**Best for:** Data-motivated readers, credibility-focused shares
**Why it works:** Promises evidence, not opinion

### SEO-optimized
> Pair Programming Benefits: How It Improved Our Code Quality and Velocity

**Characters:** 72
**Target keyword:** "pair programming benefits"
**Best for:** Organic search, evergreen ranking

### How-to practical
> How to Introduce Pair Programming Without Slowing Down Your Team

**Characters:** 62
**Best for:** Readers with implementation concerns
**Why it works:** Addresses primary objection directly

### Contrarian/Permission-giving
> Yes, Pair Programming Is Worth the Cost — Here's the Math

**Characters:** 55
**Best for:** Readers who've dismissed the idea before
**Why it works:** Acknowledges skepticism, promises justification

---

## Recommendation

**For SEO primary:** Use "Pair Programming Benefits: How It Improved Our Code Quality and Velocity" — captures search intent directly.

**For social/engagement:** Use "The Counterintuitive Reason Two Engineers Are Faster Than One" — strongest curiosity hook.

**For A/B test:** Test "Pair Programming Cut Our Bug Rate by 40%..." vs "The Counterintuitive Reason..." — contrasts proof vs. curiosity.
```

## Example 2: Email subject line variants

**Input:**
- Content: Product announcement — new collaboration feature
- Platform: Email newsletter
- Constraints: 40-50 characters (mobile preview), avoid spam triggers
- Audience: Existing customers
- Goal: Open rate + feature adoption

**Output:**

```md
# Subject line variants for: Collaboration Feature Announcement

## Content summary
Announcing real-time collaboration in the document editor, available to all Pro users.

## Primary constraints
- **Platform:** Email (mobile-first)
- **Max characters:** 50 (preview shows ~35 on mobile)
- **Primary goal:** Open rate → feature adoption
- **Target audience:** Pro customers (already paying)

---

## Variants

### Benefit-driven
> Edit together in real time — now live

**Characters:** 37
**Best for:** Users who will immediately see value
**Why it works:** Clear benefit, no fluff

### Announcement/News
> New: Real-time collaboration is here

**Characters:** 37
**Best for:** Users who follow product updates
**Why it works:** "New" signals freshness, clear topic

### Curiosity-driven
> You asked, we built it

**Characters:** 22
**Best for:** Engaged users who've requested features
**Why it works:** Personal, acknowledges community

### Urgency (soft)
> Try real-time editing with your team today

**Characters:** 43
**Best for:** Driving immediate action
**Why it works:** Action-oriented without pressure

### Personal/Conversational
> Your docs just got more collaborative

**Characters:** 37
**Best for:** Friendly brand voice
**Why it works:** "Your" makes it personal, implies value

### Problem-solution
> No more version conflicts — real-time editing is live

**Characters:** 50
**Best for:** Users frustrated by previous workflow
**Why it works:** Leads with problem they recognize

---

## Recommendation

**For open rate:** Use "You asked, we built it" — curiosity drives opens.

**For clarity:** Use "New: Real-time collaboration is here" — no ambiguity.

**Avoid:** Exclamation marks (spam filters), all caps, "free" (spam trigger).
```

## Example 3: Edge case — sensitive topic requiring careful framing

**Input:**
- Content: Article about layoffs and organizational change management
- Platform: Business publication
- Constraints: Professional tone required, no sensationalism
- Audience: HR leaders and executives
- Challenge: Topic is sensitive; headlines must be accurate without being callous

**Output:**

```md
# Headline variants for: Layoff Communication and Change Management

## Content summary
Guidance for leaders on how to communicate workforce reductions with clarity, empathy, and legal compliance.

## Primary constraints
- **Platform:** Business publication
- **Max characters:** 70
- **Primary goal:** Engagement with appropriate tone
- **Target audience:** HR leaders, executives
- **Sensitivity:** Must be professional; avoid sensationalism or callousness

---

## Variants

### Direct/Practical
> How to Communicate Workforce Reductions with Clarity and Compassion

**Characters:** 66
**Best for:** Leaders facing imminent decisions
**Why it works:** Direct, acknowledges both practical and human elements

### Problem-focused
> The Communication Mistakes Companies Make During Layoffs

**Characters:** 56
**Best for:** Leaders wanting to avoid pitfalls
**Why it works:** Learning from others' errors feels safer than "how to lay people off"

### Process-oriented
> A Framework for Navigating Workforce Transitions

**Characters:** 50
**Best for:** Strategic thinkers, euphemism-preferred contexts
**Why it works:** Professional framing, implies structure

### Employee-centered
> What Departing Employees Need to Hear — and How to Say It

**Characters:** 56
**Best for:** Empathy-driven leaders
**Why it works:** Centers affected people, signals human approach

### Leadership-focused
> Leading Through Reduction: Communication Strategies for Hard Conversations

**Characters:** 71
**Best for:** Executive audience, leadership development context
**Why it works:** Positions as leadership challenge, not just HR task

---

## Variants to avoid

| Headline | Why not |
|----------|---------|
| "How to Fire People the Right Way" | Callous framing |
| "Layoff Secrets from Top HR Leaders" | Trivializes, clickbait |
| "The Layoff Playbook" | Sounds cynical |
| "Cutting Staff Without Killing Morale" | Violent metaphor inappropriate |

---

## Recommendation

**For primary use:** "How to Communicate Workforce Reductions with Clarity and Compassion" — direct, professional, acknowledges complexity.

**For problem-aware angle:** "The Communication Mistakes Companies Make During Layoffs" — positions as learning opportunity.

**Note:** All variants reviewed for tone appropriateness given topic sensitivity.
```
